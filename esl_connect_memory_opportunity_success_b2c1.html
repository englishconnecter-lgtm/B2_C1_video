Memory, Opportunity and Success

B2/C1 video lesson about Joshua Foer, memory techniques, natural talent, opportunity, and the nature versus nurture debate.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a short video about how science journalist Joshua Foer became a memory champion. The lesson explores whether success comes mainly from natural ability, training, or access to opportunity.

Before you watch

  1. Do you think memory is mostly natural ability or a skill people can train?
  2. What techniques do you use to remember important information?
  3. How can family background or access to resources affect school success?
Key question: Could anyone become a “mental athlete” with the right training and opportunity?

B2/C1 Grammar: Contrast, Cause and Emphasis

This expanded section practises language for explaining surprising results, causes, contributing factors and the nature-versus-nurture argument.

FunctionUseful patternExample
Contrast with a noun or -ing formDespite / In spite of + noun/-ing, ...Despite being a novice, Joshua reached the final.
Contrast with a full clauseAlthough / Even though + subject + verb, ...Although he was new to the competition, he performed extremely well.
Cause... because / since / as ...He improved because he trained for hours every day.
Cause with noun phrasebecause of / due to + noun phraseIQ scores rose partly because of nutrition and technology.
Contribution... contributed to ... / played a role in ...Expert training contributed to his success.
EmphasisIt was ... that ...It was opportunity that helped explain his progress.
Balanced contrastnot ... but ...It is not only nature but also nurture that shapes outcomes.
ResultAs a result, ... / Consequently, ...As a result, he became a serious competitor.

Interactive Memory Palace Game

Practise the memory palace technique from the video. You will memorise a shopping list by connecting each item to a step in a normal morning routine.

How it works: imagine your routine clearly. Put one funny or exaggerated shopping item in each place. Then mentally walk through the routine again to remember the list in order.

Step 1: Build your routine palace

Drag each shopping item into the correct step of the routine.

  1. Wake up in bed.
  2. Brush your teeth in the bathroom.
  3. Make breakfast in the kitchen.
  4. Put on your shoes by the front door.
  5. Check your bag before leaving.
  6. Walk to the bus stop.
milk bread eggs bananas coffee toothpaste

2. Bathroom sink

3. Kitchen table

4. Front door

You try to put on your shoes, but they are full of...

5. School bag

You open your bag and find a bunch of...

6. Bus stop

At the bus stop, everyone is holding a cup of...

Step 2: Recall test

Without looking back at the palace, write the six shopping items in order.

Step 3: Create your own list

Choose six new shopping items and connect them to your own routine. Use vivid images: strange, funny, colourful, oversized, noisy or impossible.

Speaking task: Explain your routine to a partner using sequence language: First, when I wake up..., Then, in the bathroom..., After that..., Finally...
