Why Are People Superstitious? B2/C1 Lesson

B2/C1 ESL Connect Video lesson based on the tapescript: superstition, religion, cultural habit, forgotten practical advice and psychological bias.

Video link: Open on YouTube

Lesson Aim

Students watch a video about the origins of common superstitions and why irrational beliefs can survive through culture, habit and psychological bias.

Video structure: Part 1 explores religious and cultural origins of superstitions. Part 2 explains coincidences, forgotten practical advice, cultural habit and psychological bias.

Before watching

  1. Which superstitions are common in your country?
  2. Do you think superstitions are harmless?
  3. Why might people follow superstitions even if they do not believe in them?

B2/C1 Grammar: Explaining Origins and Cause

The video explains where beliefs come from and why they continue, so the grammar focus is on passive reporting, cause-and-effect language and concession.

Example: Knocking on wood is thought to come from ancient folklore.

Grammar Section 1: Passive Reporting

1. Complete: “Knocking on wood ___ to come from ancient folklore.”

2. Which sentence is correct?

Grammar Section 2: Cause and Result

1. Complete: “Because the match stayed lit too long, it ___ attention from a sniper.”

2. Which sentence clearly shows cause and result?

Grammar Section 3: Concession

1. Complete: “___ superstitions have no basis in science, they can still influence behaviour.”

2. Which sentence is most natural?

Thought-Provoking Questions

1. Are superstitions mostly harmless, or can they sometimes be harmful?

2. Why do people keep cultural habits after the original belief has disappeared?

3. Can a superstition be useful if it increases confidence?

4. Should hotels and buildings skip unlucky floor numbers?

5. How is superstition similar to cognitive bias?

6. Are sports rituals rational if they improve performance?

7. What modern beliefs might people in the future consider superstitions?

Challenge: Use phrases such as It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., and This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: Two friends are preparing for an important sports match. One insists on wearing lucky socks. The other is sceptical but wants to be respectful.

Student A

You believe your lucky ritual helps you perform better. Explain why.

Student B

You are sceptical. Ask questions about evidence, memory and confidence without being rude.

Role play task: Discuss the superstition for five minutes. By the end, agree whether the ritual should continue and why.
