Multitasking and Attention

B2/C1 video lesson on attention, conscious awareness, inattention blindness and the myth of multitasking.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a psychology video about multitasking and attention. The lesson explores why human attention is limited, how focused attention can make us miss obvious details, and why multitasking can make us inefficient or even dangerous.

Before you watch

  1. Do you think you are good at multitasking?
  2. What activities are dangerous to do at the same time?
  3. Why do people often believe they can do many things at once?

Comprehension 1

Click each question to reveal the answer.

Why does the video compare attention to a spotlight?
Because attention can be focused strongly in one direction at a time, like a beam of light.
What does the zoom lens comparison explain?
It explains that we can narrow our focus for detail or widen it to notice more things, but we cannot do both at the same time.
Why does only a small amount of information reach conscious awareness?
Because the brain receives huge amounts of sensory information but attention is limited.
What does the dinosaur example show?
It shows that when we focus on one task, we can miss surprising details that are right in front of us.
What is inattention blindness?
It is when focused attention makes us miss something visible or obvious.

Comprehension 2

Click each question to reveal the answer.

What happens in the Invisible Gorilla experiment?
People focus on counting passes and often completely miss a gorilla walking across the screen.
What happens when attention is overloaded?
We miss things and usually perform tasks less well than if we did them one at a time.
When is it possible to do two things at once?
When the two activities require different sets of cognitive resources.
Why can hands-free phone use still be dangerous while driving?
Because talking on the phone can create mental images, which use the same visual resources needed for driving.
What is the main message of the video?
The brain is not wired to do many demanding things at once; multitasking is often inefficient and can be dangerous.

B2/C1 Grammar: Modals of Possibility, Ability and Deduction

This grammar section practises modal verbs used to talk about what the brain can do, what it cannot do, and what may happen when attention is overloaded.

FunctionPatternExample
Ability / possibilitycan / be able toWe can switch attention from one task to another.
Impossibilitycannot / can’tWe can’t process everything at once.
Possibilitymay / might / couldA driver may look at a hazard but fail to see it.
Logical conclusionmustIf attention is limited, multitasking must reduce performance in some situations.
