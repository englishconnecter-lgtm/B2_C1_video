Weird British Laws: Tradition, Humour and Authority

B2/C1 video lesson on strange old laws, British traditions, public behaviour and comedy.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a humorous video in which the presenter tries to break several old or unusual British laws in public. The lesson explores outdated rules, public reactions, authority, politeness, tradition and comedy.

Before you watch

  1. Do you know any strange old laws from your country?
  2. Why do some outdated laws remain even when nobody takes them seriously?
  3. Is it ever acceptable to break a silly law for comedy?

B2/C1 Grammar: Legal and Social Permission

This grammar section practises language for laws, rules, permission and obligation.

FunctionPatternExample

Permissionbe allowed to + verbThey are legally allowed to stop you.
Obligationhave to + verbHe has to act suspiciously with the salmon.
Speculationmight / could + verbHe might get arrested if the police take it seriously.

Discussion

  1. Why do countries keep old laws that nobody follows?
  2. Should silly laws be officially removed?
  3. Is the presenter making fun of Britain, or celebrating its weirdness?
  4. Can comedy be a good way to criticise authority?
  5. What is the difference between breaking a harmful law and breaking a harmless outdated law?
  6. Would this kind of video work in your country?

Role Play: Ancient Law Review Committee

Situation: A government committee is reviewing old laws. Some members want to remove them; others think they are part of national history and tradition.

Student A: Legal reformer

Argue that outdated laws are confusing, ridiculous and should be removed from the legal system.

Student B: Tradition defender

Argue that old laws are harmless, historically interesting and part of national culture.
