B2/C1 ESL Connect Video lesson based on the tapescript: persuasive arguments, emotional appeal, faulty reasoning, political debate and evaluating claims.

Video link: Open on YouTube

Lesson Aim

Students watch a video about logical fallacies and learn how persuasive language can appeal to emotions, simplify complex issues and make weak arguments sound convincing.

Video structure: Part 1 introduces debate, persuasion and the first three fallacies. Part 2 explains personal attacks, false dilemmas and strawman arguments, then connects these skills to informed voting.

  2. Can an argument sound convincing but still be illogical?

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups. Give reasons, examples and possible consequences.

1. Which logical fallacy do you see most often in politics or social media?

2. Can emotional appeals be ethical, or are they always manipulative?

3. Why are simple stories so powerful in debates about complex issues?

4. Should schools teach students how to identify faulty arguments?

5. Is it worse to use a personal attack or a strawman argument?

6. How can voters check whether a politician is presenting facts fairly?

7. Can a speaker use a fallacy accidentally, or is it usually deliberate?

Challenge: Choose one question and prepare a one-minute answer using cautious language such as: It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: Two candidates are debating whether teenagers should be allowed to vote in local elections. One candidate uses several weak arguments.

Student A

You are Candidate 1. Use one strong argument and one weak argument. Try to sound persuasive.

Student B

You are Candidate 2. Identify the weak argument politely and reframe the issue more logically.

Role play task: Debate for five minutes. By the end, identify at least two fallacies and rewrite one weak argument as a stronger one.
