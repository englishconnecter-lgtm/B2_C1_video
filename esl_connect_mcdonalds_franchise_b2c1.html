McDonald’s Franchise: Big Sales, Small Margins?

B2/C1 business video lesson on franchising, investment, fees, profit and revenue.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a business video about the McDonald’s franchise model. The lesson explores investment, franchise fees, sales, profit margins, company revenue and whether buying a franchise is as profitable as people imagine.

Before you watch

  1. Why do entrepreneurs buy franchises instead of starting their own brands?
  2. What costs might a franchise owner have to pay?
  3. Would you rather own one restaurant or the brand behind thousands of restaurants?

Comprehension 2

Click each question to reveal the answer.

How much of the initial investment must be cash or non-borrowed assets?
Forty percent of the initial investment must be cash or non-borrowed assets.
How much does the average McDonald’s restaurant take in each year in sales?
The video says it takes in around $2.7 million a year in sales.
What is the estimated yearly profit for most franchisees?
Most franchisees pull in roughly $150,000 a year.
What fees does McDonald’s charge franchise owners?
They pay a $45,000 franchise fee, a 4% monthly service fee and an average rental fee of about 10.7% of sales.
Why is franchising so profitable for McDonald’s corporation?
Because franchise owners pay for the locations upfront, while McDonald’s keeps a large share of the revenue paid to it with lower operating costs.

B2/C1 Grammar: Financial Comparisons and Concession

This grammar section practises language for comparing revenue, profit and business costs.

FunctionPatternExample
ConcessionAlthough / While + clauseAlthough sales are high, profits may be much lower.
Percentage comparisononly / around / roughly + percentA profit of $150,000 is only around 6% of sales.
Cause and effectOnce + costs are deducted,...Once costs are deducted, the owner is left with less profit.
ContrastOn the surface..., but...On the surface, company-owned stores look more profitable, but they cost more to run.
