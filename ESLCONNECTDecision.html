Students watch a video about decision fatigue and discuss how repeated choices can affect judgement, confidence and performance in both everyday and high-stakes contexts.

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups. Give reasons, examples and possible consequences.

1. Should courts schedule important decisions only when judges are most mentally fresh?

2. How fair is it if the time of day can influence a life-changing decision?

3. Should doctors, judges and pilots have stricter limits on long shifts?

4. Is decision fatigue mainly an individual problem or an institutional problem?

5. What daily choices could people remove to protect their mental energy?

6. Can too many options make people less free rather than more free?

7. Which decisions in your life deserve the most energy, and which ones deserve less?

Challenge: Choose one question and prepare a one-minute answer using cautious language such as: It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: A hospital or court is reviewing whether staff should work shorter shifts and whether important decisions should be scheduled earlier in the day.

Student A

Argue that institutional changes are necessary to reduce decision fatigue and protect people affected by high-stakes decisions.

Student B

Argue that shorter shifts and schedule changes are expensive, difficult to organise and may create new problems.

Role play task: Discuss the proposal for five minutes. By the end, agree on two practical changes and one possible drawback.
