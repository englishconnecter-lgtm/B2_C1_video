Personality Tests: Useful Insight or Bad Science?

B2/C1 video lesson on personality testing, self-reporting, bias, reliability and opportunity.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a video questioning the reliability and impact of personality tests such as the MBTI, Big Five, DiSC, Process Communication Model and the Enneagram. The lesson explores test design, self-reporting, bias, changing definitions and the risks of using personality tests in schools and companies.

Before you watch

  1. Have you ever taken a personality test? Did it feel accurate?
  2. Should companies use personality tests when hiring?
  3. Is personality fixed, or can it change over time?

Click each question to reveal the answer.

Who developed the MBTI questionnaire?
Katherine Cook Briggs and Isabel Briggs Myers developed it in 1942.
What does the MBTI do?
It classifies people’s personalities into 16 types.
What main criticism does the video make about personality tests?
It argues that they do not actually reveal stable truths about personality.
Why are self-reported tests problematic?
People can lie, and even with good intentions, objective self-evaluation is difficult.
Why are forced-choice questions problematic?
People may value both options, and the same person may answer differently when asked the same question again.

Comprehension 2

Click each question to reveal the answer.

What did one study find about people taking the Myers-Briggs a second time?
Nearly half of people who took it again five weeks later were assigned a different type.
What problem is created by strict personality categories?
People with very similar scores can be placed in different categories, which does not reflect real-life nuance.
How have definitions of introvert and extrovert changed?
They have shifted over time, from Jung’s definitions to ideas about shyness, outgoingness and energy from alone time or social interaction.
What does research increasingly suggest about personality?
It suggests that personality can shift during key periods, and some traits are malleable.
Why is institutional use of personality tests risky?
Schools and companies may use results to limit people’s study, career or hiring opportunities, even though the tests do not predict role performance well.
