Lesson Overview

Students watch a video about self-driving cars and ethical dilemmas. The lesson explores autonomous vehicles, programmed decisions, harm reduction, targeting algorithms, responsibility and technology ethics.

Before you watch

  1. Should a self-driving car protect its passenger first, or minimise total harm?
  2. Who should decide how autonomous vehicles behave in accidents: programmers, companies or governments?
  3. Is a random decision ever fairer than a programmed ethical rule?

Thought-Provoking Questions

  1. If a self-driving car has to choose between protecting its passenger and protecting strangers, whose safety should come first?
  2. Is it fair for a car’s algorithm to treat people differently based on factors such as vehicle size, helmet use or passenger safety ratings?
  3. Should customers be allowed to buy a car programmed to save them “at any cost”?
  4. If a self-driving car causes harm because of its programming, who should be responsible?
  5. Could a random decision ever be more ethical than a carefully programmed decision?
  6. Should technology always try to minimise total harm, even if that means sacrificing one individual?
  7. What other technologies are creating ethical dilemmas before society has agreed on clear rules?

Role Play: Government Committee on Self-Driving Cars

Situation: A government committee is deciding ethical rules for self-driving cars. Student A argues that the car should minimise total harm. Student B argues that this could unfairly target certain people and that some decisions should be random or regulated by law.

Student A: Harm Reduction Advocate

Defend the principle of minimising harm. Explain why reducing total deaths and injuries should guide the technology.

Student B: Fairness and Responsibility Advocate

Challenge the principle. Explain why targeting, discrimination and responsibility make the issue more complicated.
