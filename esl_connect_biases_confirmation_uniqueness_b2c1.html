Biases: How They Shape Our View of Reality

B2/C1 video lesson on bias, confirmation bias, uniqueness bias and perspective.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students watch a Big Think video about bias, confirmation bias and uniqueness bias. The lesson explores why biases are not simply bad, how they help us make sense of the world, and how becoming aware of them can broaden our perspective.

Before you watch

  1. What does the word “bias” mean to you?
  2. Can bias ever be useful?
  3. Why is it difficult to notice our own biases?

How does the speaker define bias in a broad sense?
He says biases guide us and help us avoid harm, such as preferring solid ground or certain foods.
Why does the speaker say we should not simply try to remove biases?
Because biases are essential; the goal is to recognise them rather than see them only as negative.
How can biases affect clear thinking?
They can cloud our thinking and twist reality so that it confirms our own view of the world.

Comprehension 2

Click each question to reveal the answer.

What example does the speaker give to explain confirmation bias?
After thinking about buying a particular car, you suddenly notice that car everywhere.
Why does the speaker say some confirmation bias is essential?
Because it helps us piece together a coherent world; without it, we could feel lost.
How can awareness of confirmation bias help in discussions or across cultures?
It helps us recognise that other people see the world differently and that our view is not automatically better.
Why does the speaker find uniqueness bias amusing?
Because we see ourselves and people close to us as especially unique because we have much more information about them.
What creates circles of uniqueness?
The more we know about people and the more important they are in our lives, the more unique they seem to us.
