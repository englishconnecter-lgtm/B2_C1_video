Vocabulary Review

ExpressionMeaningExample
brain-friendly feedbackfeedback that the other person can process without becoming defensiveA micro-yes makes feedback more brain-friendly.
micro-yesa short permission question before feedback“Can I share an idea with you?” is a micro-yes.
data pointa specific thing you saw or heard“You said you’d send the email by 11” is a data point.
blur wordsvague words that mean different things to different people“Reliable” and “proactive” can be blur words.
impact statementa sentence explaining the effect of the behaviour“Because I didn’t get the message, I was blocked on my work.”

B2/C1 Grammar: Giving Specific and Diplomatic Feedback

This video uses practical workplace language, so the grammar focus is on permission questions, specific data points and cause-and-effect impact statements.

Example: Do you have five minutes to talk about how that last conversation went?

Grammar Section 1: Permission Questions

1. Which sentence is a good micro-yes?

2. Complete: “Do you have five minutes ___ how that last conversation went?”

Grammar Section 2: Specific Data Points

1. Which sentence is most specific?

2. Which sentence avoids blur words?

Grammar Section 3: Impact Statements

1. Complete: “Because I didn’t get the message, I ___ on my work.”

2. Which sentence creates joint problem-solving?

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups.

1. Why do many people become defensive when receiving feedback?

2. Is it better for feedback to be direct, indirect, or somewhere in between?

3. Why are vague words like “unreliable” or “proactive” often unhelpful?

4. How can positive feedback be made more useful?

5. Should employees ask for feedback regularly, even when they are doing well?

6. What is the difference between compliance and commitment?

7. Can this four-part formula work in personal relationships too?

Challenge: Use phrases such as It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., and This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: A team member missed a deadline and it blocked your work. You need to give clear feedback without making the person defensive.

Student A

You give the feedback. Use the four-part formula: micro-yes, data point, impact statement and question.

Student B

You receive the feedback. Respond naturally, ask one clarification question and suggest a next step.

Role play task: Discuss the situation for five minutes. By the end, agree on one practical action for next time.
