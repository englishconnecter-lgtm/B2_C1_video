The Right Idea at the Wrong Time

B2/C1 video lesson on invention, timing, failure, innovation and persistence.

Open video on YouTube

Lesson Overview

Students explore why some inventions succeed while others fail, even when the original idea is brilliant. The lesson looks at Douglas Engelbart and the mouse, Stephanie Kwolek and Kevlar, Morton Heilig and early virtual reality, and Wilson Greatbatch and the pacemaker.

Before you watch

  1. Can a good idea fail because it arrives too early?
  2. Why do some inventors become famous while others are forgotten?
  3. Is failure always a bad thing in innovation?

B2/C1 Grammar: Third Conditionals and Mixed Conditionals

This grammar section uses the stories from the video to practise unreal past situations and their possible consequences.

StructureUseExample
Third conditionalimagining a different past resultIf Xerox had kept control of the idea, Apple might not have relaunched the mouse so successfully.
Mixed conditional: past condition + present resultimagining how a different past would affect the presentIf Heilig had found investors, he might be remembered as a founder of modern VR.
Mixed conditional: present condition + past resultimagining how a general present truth could have changed the pastIf companies were more open to strange ideas, the Sensorama might have succeeded.

Role Play: Pitching an Idea Before Its Time

Situation: One student is an inventor with a brilliant but unusual idea. The other student is an investor who is not sure the market is ready.

Student A: Inventor

Explain what your invention does, why it matters and what could happen if investors support it now.

Student B: Investor

Ask difficult questions about timing, cost, demand, risk and whether customers are ready.

Challenge

Use at least two third conditionals or mixed conditionals during the role play.
