B2/C1 business lesson on ultra-fast fashion, supply chains, labour concerns and tax rules.

Lesson Overview

Students watch a business video about Shein’s fast-fashion model. The lesson explores ultra-low prices, micro trends, small-batch production, real-time data, supplier networks, labour allegations, tax exemptions and brand reputation.

Before you watch

  1. Why has fast fashion become so popular?
  2. What makes very cheap clothing attractive to consumers?
  3. What problems can fast fashion create for workers and the environment?

Comprehension 1

Click each question to reveal the answer.

What is Shein known for?
It is known for extremely cheap fast fashion, such as $2 T-shirts and $7 pants.
How quickly did Shein’s sales grow during the pandemic?
The video says the brand’s sales grew sixfold in two years.
How many units of each new product does Shein initially produce?
Shein initially produces around 100 to 200 units of each new product.
How does Shein test whether products are popular?
It monitors user engagement, such as hovering, clicking and adding items to carts.
How does Shein’s model differ from traditional retail?
Traditional retailers may order 10,000 to 100,000 copies of a garment months in advance, while Shein starts with small batches and scales up if demand is strong.

Comprehension 2

Click each question to reveal the answer.

How does Shein support its on-demand model?
It uses thousands of smaller manufacturers and an ordering platform that works in real time.
What cost benefit does Shein claim from reducing excess inventory?
Shein says it saves around 30 to 40% in cost of goods sold on garments.
Why do environmental critics still worry about waste?
They argue that cheap clothing encourages people to buy more and retire clothing more quickly, creating waste after consumption.
What labour concerns are mentioned?
The video mentions accusations of worker abuse, involuntary labour cases in audits and questions about supply chain transparency.
What is the de minimis rule?
It is a U.S. tax rule allowing shipments under $800 sent to individual buyers to enter tariff-free.

B2/C1 Grammar: Passive Voice and Business Reporting

This grammar section practises reporting business facts, allegations and company actions in a formal style.

FunctionPatternExample
Reporting company valuebe valued at / reach a valuation ofShein reached a valuation of $66 billion.
Reporting accusationsbe accused of + -ingThe company has been accused of poor labour practices.
Reporting official findingsbe found to / be reported toThe audits were reported to cover part of the supplier base.
Reporting changebe trying to / be moving toShein is trying to diversify its supply chain.
